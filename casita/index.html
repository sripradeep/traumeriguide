On Foot

Sedona Hikes

Red rock trails for every energy level, all within a short drive.

From a flat half-mile stroll to a strenuous summit push, Sedona's trail system has something for every energy level — and several of the best trailheads are a 10–15 minute drive from the casitas. A $5 Red Rock Pass (or America the Beautiful pass) is required to park at most Forest Service trailheads.

Insider Tip

Start early. Devil's Bridge, Cathedral Rock, and Soldier Pass fill their trailhead lots by 8–9am most of the year. Download the AllTrails app before you go for live difficulty ratings, water-crossing conditions, and closure alerts — and carry more water than you think you'll need; the high desert air is deceptively dry.

Sacred Ground

Vortexes & Spiritual Sites

Four famous energy vortexes, plus two quieter places for reflection.

Long before Sedona became a New Age destination, the Yavapai and Hopi considered this red-rock landscape sacred, using it for healing, ceremony, and vision quests. Today the area is best known for its four vortex sites — places where many visitors report a noticeable shift in energy, mood, or clarity — along with a few quieter spots built for reflection.

Insider Tip

Vortex etiquette: stay on marked trails, never remove rocks or build cairns, and skip the urge to light candles, burn sage, or leave offerings — these sites are sacred to Indigenous communities and the National Forest prohibits open flame and disturbing natural features. Sunrise and sunset are the quietest, most powerful times to visit any of the four vortexes.

State parks, ancient ruins, and only-in-Sedona experiences.

Beyond the trailheads, Sedona's red-rock setting holds state parks, ancient cliff dwellings, classic photo stops, and a couple of only-in-Sedona experiences worth building a day around.

Scenic Drives

Big views without leaving the car — from a quick loop to a half-day out-and-back.

Some of Sedona's best scenery doesn't require leaving the car. These routes range from a quick 20-minute loop to a full half-day out-and-back, and several of the biggest views in Arizona are visible right from the pull-offs.

Insider Tip

Schnebly Hill Road requires a high-clearance (ideally 4WD) vehicle past the first mile or so — a standard sedan should turn around at the first overlook. For the easiest big payoff with zero off-roading, drive the Red Rock Scenic Byway (SR-179) at golden hour, when the rock formations turn deep red-orange.

Before You Go

Guest Tips

Everything else worth knowing to make the most of your stay in Sedona.

Getting Around

A car is recommended for trailheads and day trips, though West Sedona (ChocolaTree, Vespa Cafe, Whole Foods) is genuinely walkable from the casitas. Download the AllTrails app before hiking for real-time conditions and closures.

Red Rock Pass

Most Forest Service trailheads (Devil's Bridge, Cathedral Rock, Bell Rock, Boynton Canyon, and more) require a $5 day pass or $15 weekly pass, sold at trailhead kiosks and local visitor centers. An America the Beautiful annual pass also works.

Best Time to Visit

March–May and September–November bring the most comfortable hiking temperatures. Summer days run hot (90s°F) but evenings cool off pleasantly at Sedona's ~4,500 ft elevation — start hikes by 7am in summer.

Quiet Hours

In line with City of Sedona ordinances, please keep noise to a minimum between 9:00 PM and 8:00 AM — both casitas sit in a residential compound shared with neighbors.

Wildlife Note

Javelina, coyotes, and the occasional rattlesnake are present in the area, especially around dusk — keep a respectful distance and keep pets leashed outdoors.
